# Big Idea 5: Impact of Computing — Essential Knowledge

### Topic 5.1 — Beneficial and Harmful Effects
* **IOC-1.A.1** People create computing innovations.
* **IOC-1.A.2** The way people complete tasks often changes to incorporate new computing innovations.
* **IOC-1.A.3** Not every effect of a computing innovation is anticipated in advance.
* **IOC-1.A.4** A single effect can be viewed as both beneficial and harmful by different people, or even by the same person.
* **IOC-1.A.5** Advances in computing have generated and increased creativity in other fields, such as medicine, engineering, communications, and the arts.
* **IOC-1.B.1** Computing innovations can be used in ways not originally intended (e.g., WWW, targeted advertising, machine learning/data mining).
* **IOC-1.B.2** Some uses of computing innovations may have harmful impacts on society, economy, or culture.
* **IOC-1.B.3** Responsible programmers try to consider unintended uses and their potential effects.
* **IOC-1.B.4** It is not possible for a programmer to consider all possible uses of a computing innovation.
* **IOC-1.B.5** Computing innovations have often had unintended beneficial effects, leading to advances in other fields.
* **IOC-1.B.6** Rapid sharing or wide use of programs can create significant impacts beyond the control of the programmer.

### Topic 5.2 — Digital Divide

* **IOC-1.C.1** Internet access varies between socioeconomic, geographic, and demographic characteristics, as well as between countries.
* **IOC-1.C.2** The “digital divide” refers to differing access to computing devices and the Internet.
* **IOC-1.C.3** The digital divide can affect both groups and individuals.
* **IOC-1.C.4** The digital divide raises issues of equity, access, and influence, both globally and locally.
* **IOC-1.C.5** The digital divide is affected by the actions of individuals, organizations, and governments.

---

### Topic 5.3 — Computing Bias

* **IOC-1.D.1** Computing innovations can reflect human biases, through biased algorithms or biased data.
* **IOC-1.D.2** Programmers should take action to reduce bias in algorithms.
* **IOC-1.D.3** Biases can be embedded at all levels of software development.

---

### Topic 5.4 — Crowdsourcing

* **IOC-1.E.1** Widespread access to information and public data facilitates identifying problems, developing solutions, and sharing results.
* **IOC-1.E.2** Science has been affected by distributed and citizen science.
* **IOC-1.E.3** Citizen science = research conducted in whole/part by distributed individuals (often non-scientists) contributing data via their computing devices.
* **IOC-1.E.4** Crowdsourcing is obtaining input or information from a large number of people via the Internet.
* **IOC-1.E.5** Human capabilities can be enhanced by collaboration via computing.
* **IOC-1.E.6** Crowdsourcing enables new collaboration models (e.g., funding for businesses or social causes).

### Topic 5.5 — Legal and Ethical Concerns

* **IOC-1.F.1** Material created on a computer is the intellectual property of the creator or an organization.
* **IOC-1.F.2** Ease of access/distribution of digitized information raises IP concerns about ownership, value, and use.
* **IOC-1.F.3** Measures should be taken to safeguard intellectual property.
* **IOC-1.F.4** Using someone else’s material without permission and presenting it as your own is plagiarism and may have legal consequences.
* **IOC-1.F.5** Legal reuse methods:
  * Creative Commons
  * Open source
  * Open access
* **IOC-1.F.6** Use of others’ material should always be cited.
* **IOC-1.F.7** Creative Commons, open source, and open access enable broad access to digital information.
* **IOC-1.F.8** Using computing to harm individuals/groups raises legal and ethical concerns.
* **IOC-1.F.9** Computing can play roles in social and political issues, raising legal/ethical concerns.
* **IOC-1.F.10** The digital divide raises ethical concerns around computing.
* **IOC-1.F.11** Examples of legal/ethical concerns:
  * software enabling digital media downloads/streaming
  * biased algorithms
  * devices that continuously collect/analyze user data

### Topic 5.6 — Safe Computing
* **IOC-2.A.1** Personally identifiable information (PII) = information that identifies, links, or describes a person (e.g., SSN, age, race, phone, medical, financial, biometrics).
* **IOC-2.A.2** Search engines can record/maintain user search history.
* **IOC-2.A.3** Websites can record/maintain page-view histories.
* **IOC-2.A.4** Devices, websites, and networks can collect user location data.
* **IOC-2.A.5** Technology enables collection and use of information about/for individuals, groups, and institutions.
* **IOC-2.A.6** Search engines can use search history for suggestions/targeted marketing.
* **IOC-2.A.7** Data like geolocation, cookies, and browsing history can be aggregated into knowledge about individuals.
* **IOC-2.A.8** PII/information online can enhance user experiences.
* **IOC-2.A.9** PII stored online can simplify purchases.
* **IOC-2.A.10** Commercial/government curation of information can be exploited if protections are ignored.
* **IOC-2.A.11** Information online can be used in unintended ways (e.g., forwarded emails, retweets, employer screening).
* **IOC-2.A.12** PII can be used for stalking, identity theft, or aiding crimes.
* **IOC-2.A.13** Once online, information is difficult to delete.
* **IOC-2.A.14** Programs can track and record user location/movement/time spent.
* **IOC-2.A.15** Social media posts can be combined with other data to deduce private information.
* **IOC-2.B.1** Authentication protects devices/information (e.g., strong passwords, multifactor authentication).
* **IOC-2.B.2** A strong password is memorable for the user but difficult for others to guess.
* **IOC-2.B.3** Multifactor authentication requires two or more evidence types (knowledge, possession, inherence).
* **IOC-2.B.4** Each authentication factor adds another layer of security.
* **IOC-2.B.5** Encryption encodes data to prevent unauthorized access. Two approaches:
  * Symmetric key encryption (same key for encryption/decryption).
  * Public key encryption (public key for encryption, private key for decryption).
* **IOC-2.B.6** Certificate authorities issue digital certificates validating ownership of encryption keys.
* **IOC-2.B.7** Antivirus/malware scanners help protect systems.
* **IOC-2.B.8** A computer virus = malicious program that copies itself and gains unauthorized access.
* **IOC-2.B.9** Malware = software intended to damage/control a system.
* **IOC-2.B.10** All real-world systems have flaws; updates help fix vulnerabilities.
* **IOC-2.B.11** Users can control program permissions to protect privacy.
* **IOC-2.C.1** Phishing tricks users into providing personal info.
* **IOC-2.C.2** Keylogging records keystrokes to steal data.
* **IOC-2.C.3** Data on public networks can be intercepted, analyzed, modified (e.g., rogue access points).
* **IOC-2.C.4** Rogue access points provide unauthorized access to secure networks.
* **IOC-2.C.5** Malicious links can be disguised in webpages or emails.
* **IOC-2.C.6** Emails, attachments, and links can compromise systems.
* **IOC-2.C.7** Untrustworthy freeware/shareware downloads may contain malware.
